Published Books

Transitory Gardens, Uprooted Lives

Yale University Press, 1993.

With text by Morton's Yale colleague, landscape architect Diana Balmori, Transitory Gardens, Uprooted Lives attends to gardens built by New York City unhoused people. The gardeners in this book circumvent the law to appropriate public space on empty lots and behind squatted buildings, evading the more rigid order of city-sanctioned community gardens. Spanning a wide swath of the Lower East Side, the images within consider how each space might reflect its caretaker's desire for basic comforts.

148 pages, 112 photographs.

The Tunnel

Yale University Press, 1995.
Published in German translation as Der Tunnel.

The Tunnel examines the lives of some of New York City's homeless citizens, who have set up a unique community in an Amtrak tunnel under the West Side Highway.

Fragile Dwelling

Aperture Foundation, 2000.

This is the third volume of photographs and text by Margaret Morton documenting the lives and living spaces of New York City's homeless population. Over a ten-year period, Margaret Morton documented the inventive ways in which homeless people in New York City have created not only places to live but also communities that offer a sense of pride, place, and individuality.

xx pages, xx photographs.

Glass House

Penn State University Press, 2004.

The photographs and oral histories in Glass House document the resilience of thirty-five squatters, many still in their teens, who defied the law to occupy a derelict glass factory on New York City’s Lower East Side. Homeless, unable or unwilling to return to their families, they formed a community and, with determination and ingenuity, reshaped their lives as well as the building.

xx pages, xx photographs.

Cities of the Dead: The Ancestral Cemeteries of Kyrgyzstan

University of Washington Press, 2014.

Cities of the Dead emerged from Morton's 2006 travels in Kyrgyzstan with her friend Virlana Tkacz, founding director of the Yara Arts Group, a resident company at La MaMa Experimental Theatre Club in New York. Captivated by Kyrgyz ancestral cemeteries she saw on that trip, Morton made return visits to shoot digital photographs at representative sites across the country between 2006 and 2008. The images in this book present distinct cultural and religious juxtapositions, with animist and shamanistic motifs appearing alongside Islamic and Sufic iconography, along with the Russian and Soviet influence of more recent decades.
